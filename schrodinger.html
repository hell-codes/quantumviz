Schrödinger's Cat Thought Experiment

Erwin Schrödinger's 1935 thought experiment reveals the strangeness of quantum superposition when applied to macroscopic objects.

Quantum Superposition

|ψ⟩ = α|Alive⟩ + β|Dead⟩
|ψ⟩ — the quantum state of the system
α, β — probability amplitudes (complex numbers)
|α|² — probability of finding cat alive
|β|² — probability of finding cat dead

Before measurement: |α|² + |β|² = 1 (normalisation)
After measurement: state collapses to one of the two.

Before the box is opened, the system exists in both states simultaneously. Observation forces a definite outcome — wavefunction collapse.

Physical Interpretation

• The box represents a closed quantum system.

• The radioactive atom has a configurable probability of decaying (set with the slider).

• If decay occurs, the poison is released and the cat dies.

• If no decay occurs, the cat remains alive.

• Before measurement, the system exists in a superposition of both possibilities.

• When the box is opened, the wavefunction collapses to a single definite state.

• Run many experiments to observe how the outcome statistics converge to the decay probability you set.

Why This Thought Experiment Matters

Schrödinger designed this to highlight the paradox between quantum theory and classical intuition. While superposition works perfectly for microscopic particles, applying it to macroscopic objects like cats reveals profound questions about when and why quantum systems collapse.

This remains one of the deepest open questions in the foundations of quantum mechanics — connecting to interpretations such as Copenhagen, Many-Worlds, and Decoherence theory.
